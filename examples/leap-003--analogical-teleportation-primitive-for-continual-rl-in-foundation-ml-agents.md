## The Leap

Current continual reinforcement learning (CRL) systems in 2025–2026 — even the advanced ones operating on or alongside foundation models — are still overwhelmingly solving the stability-plasticity dilemma *from inside the RL paradigm*.

They rely on replay buffers, regularization (e.g. CKA-RL style knowledge adaptation), dynamic world models, hypernetworks, or parameter-efficient updates when facing non-stationary tasks. These are valuable, but they remain local, incremental adaptations.

The non-obvious structural recombination is this:

**Insert a dedicated Analogical Teleportation stage as one of the very first operations when a foundation RL agent detects significant distribution shift or plasticity loss.**

Instead of (or before) defaulting to local gradient steps, replay, or within-RL regularization:

1. The agent queries its attached portable personal context pack (researcher’s own Literature/ notes, open loops, and gaps — the Screenpipe-to-Obsidian output) + a broad slice of ML literature via SPECTER2-proximal but deliberately distant-field sampling.
2. The personal pack functions as the grounding bias and relevance filter — exactly like Caitlin Burke having the single visible ‘L’ plus her lifetime of pattern recognition.
3. High-distance but structurally analogous patterns are teleported in (e.g. contrastive self-supervised representation learning from continual vision → dynamic reward-embedding spaces for non-stationary RL; manifold regularization and embedding techniques from meta-learning / continual CL → policy "jump" operators instead of slow local adaptation; world-model surprise signals from one subfield → better detection of when teleportation is warranted).

This turns the agent from a sophisticated local optimizer into something closer to a Caitlin-style open-world reasoner: from one visible structural clue in the new environment + the researcher’s accumulated personal "L" (their own vault), it can leap to an elegant cross-domain solution in one bound.

## Why This Is the Caitlin Move (Not Incremental Improvement)

The conservative path in CRL for foundation agents is "more replay, better regularization, bigger world models, more compute on the current task distribution."

The bold structural recognition (true to the founding charter of this repo): the highest-leverage move is to give the agent the same capability that produced this repo in the first place — the ability to make high-distance analogical leaps from minimal personal clues + the structure of the broader literature, on demand.

The personal context pack is not an afterthought. It is the steering mechanism that prevents generic, low-relevance teleportations and makes the leaps personally useful to the researcher who owns the vault.

## Concrete, Low-Cost Falsification Protocol (< 2 weeks for a motivated researcher)

1. Choose a small continual RL benchmark with clear non-stationarity (e.g., a MuJoCo task sequence or Procgen-style domain shifts).
2. Create a minimal "personal context pack" (3–8 short notes on related ML/CL ideas the researcher actually cares about, or excerpts from their own vault).
3. Implement a minimal teleportation operator:
   - On detected shift (churn > threshold or world-model surprise), embed the current situation + the personal pack.
   - Retrieve SPECTER2-proximal papers from deliberately distant ML subfields (contrastive learning, meta-learning embeddings, continual vision manifolds, etc.).
   - Use the council-style Generator to propose 1–2 teleported mechanisms (e.g., contrastive-style dynamic reward embedding).
   - Run Critic pass (novelty + groundedness + actionability).
4. Compare against strong 2025 baseline (CKA-RL or Online World Model planner) on:
   - Adaptation speed after shift
   - Retention of performance on previous tasks (plasticity)
   - Final reward after several shifts

If the teleportation-augmented agent does not show clear gains on at least two of the three metrics (especially when the personal pack is high-quality), the hypothesis is weakened for this class of architectures.

## Residual Doubts (Critic Lens)

- Current foundation models already have broad knowledge; the marginal value of explicit external teleportation may be smaller than expected once models are large enough.
- Noisy or low-quality personal context packs could steer the agent toward irrelevant or harmful analogies.
- Evaluation is hard — the real test is whether the teleported ideas lead to measurably better real-world behavior over long horizons, not just benchmark scores.
- Implementation complexity and latency of the teleportation stage could outweigh benefits in fast control loops.

These are exactly the uncertainties that require the first 5–10 real runs (with human oversight) before any claim of general superiority.
